## Design Context

### Users
Primary users are Barça supporters who want a serious, football-native publication rather than churn, rumor noise, or fan-forum theater.

Reading context is split across both mobile and desktop, but mobile is the clear priority at roughly 70–75% of traffic. That means the experience should feel composed and intentional on a phone first: strong hierarchy, clean pacing, confident typography, and modules that scan quickly without collapsing into a generic card feed.

Users are coming for:
- current matchweek context
- clear editorial judgment
- club memory and atmosphere that still cash out in football reality
- a reading experience that feels like a publication, not a content product

### Brand Personality
Core brand words:
- sharp
- nocturnal
- cultured

The publication should feel emotionally inside the club, but intellectually above the churn.
It should read like an intelligent supporters’ paper and a matchweek companion for culers.
The tone should feel controlled, football-native, and serious, with contemporary magazine confidence rather than startup polish or generic sports-site energy.

Locked public voice anchors:
- “Less noise. More Barça.”
- “Match context, club memory, cleaner judgment.”

### Aesthetic Direction
The preferred exploration space is the overlap between:
- contemporary magazine / culture paper
- premium night-match companion

This means:
- publication energy over dashboard energy
- strong editorial hierarchy over feed density
- atmosphere and tension without cinematic gimmickry
- elegance, darkness, and match-night mood when useful
- enough flexibility to support both light and dark directions

Strong anti-references:
- generic sports site
- fan forum
- startup SaaS
- luxury fashion magazine
- ESPN / The Athletic clone
- dark crypto dashboard

The design should feel like a real football publication with taste: image-aware, typographically led, and emotionally charged without becoming loud.

### Design Principles
1. Publication first, sports UI second.
   Every surface should feel edited and composed, not like a feed or utility dashboard.

2. Mobile hierarchy matters most.
   Because most traffic is on mobile, typography, spacing, art direction, and module order must feel deliberate on a phone before scaling up.

3. Night-match mood without design clichés.
   Premium evening atmosphere is welcome, but avoid glow-heavy tech aesthetics, crypto-dark gradients, or empty cinematic tricks.

4. Typography and composition carry the identity.
   Distinction should come from editorial rhythm, type, cropping, and pacing — not decorative effects piled on top.

5. Current football relevance stays visible.
   Even when the interface feels cultured or archival, it must still read as a live Barça publication rooted in the present tense.

6. No generic sports-web tropes.
   Avoid scorebug aesthetics, noisy ticker logic, endless card repetition, tabloid urgency, and template-like article rails.
